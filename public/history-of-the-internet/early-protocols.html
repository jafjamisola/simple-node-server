J. C. R. Licklider had the chance to make history when he was hired by Jack Ruina, a man from the United States Department of Defense, under DARPA.

In 1964, he left the project which was five years before the ARPANET went live. It was his dream of realizing universal networking that gave the motivation that led people such as Lawrence Roberts and Robert Taylor to further the ARPANET development. Licklider later returned to lead the IPTO in 1973 for two years.

The first ARPANET link was connected between the University of California, Los Angeles and Stanford Research Institute on October 29, 1969. On December 5 the same year, the University of Utah and University of California, Santa Barbara was added to the network.

NPL

"In 1965, Donald Davies of the National Physical Laboratory (United Kingdom) proposed a national data network based on packet-switching. The proposal was not taken up nationally, but by 1970 he had designed and built the Mark I packet-switched network to meet the needs of the multidisciplinary laboratory and prove the technology under operational conditions. By 1976 12 computers and 75 terminal devices were attached and more were added until the network was replaced in 1986."

UUCP and Usenet

At Duke University, Tom Truscott and Jim Ellis, thought up the idea of using Bourne shell scripts to transfer news and messages on a serial line UUCP connection with universities near theirs, namely: University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, circa 1979. UUCPnet, as it would be named later on, also made links and provided gateways between FidoNet and dial-up BBS hosts.
